This skill captures the substantive knowledge behind LOWI Solutions' offering: how the Deferred Payment Program works, the intellectual frameworks that support it, the terminology decisions that have been made, and the proof points that back it up. It is the product of extensive iteration with Conrad and reflects his voice and strategic intent.

Use this alongside the `lowi-brand-guidelines` skill when creating branded deliverables. This skill handles substance; brand guidelines handles visual identity and tone.

## Terminology Rules

These apply across all LOWI content unless the specific audience context calls for different framing:

**Default: avoid the word "lease"** (lease, leasing, lease-to-own, leaseback, etc.) when describing LOWI's overall offering to government audiences. This is primarily because certain governments, especially the United States, have legal restrictions on leases that make the term a liability in procurement conversations at the wrapper level. Use instead:
- "Deferred Payment Program" (DPP); the primary term for LOWI's offering at the wrapper level
- "dry finance structure"; when describing the underlying mechanics generally
- "pay-to-own agreement" or "pay-to-own arrangement"; when explaining the ownership transfer
- "dry capital structure"; when describing the capital arrangement specifically
- "program term"; not "lease term"
- "title transfer"; not "lease maturity"

**Carve-outs where "lease" is appropriate.** The DPP is the wrapper; the underlying legal form is whatever the contract specifies. "Lease" appears in at least four specific contexts and should be used accurately in each:
- **Legal construct references inside the DPP wrapper**: When presenting a menu of underlying legal structures in an RFI, RFP, RFQ, Sources Sought, or other formal tender response, the actual structure names are terms of art and should be used directly: Lease-Purchase, Finance/Capital Lease, and Operating Lease. See the DPP Structural Options section. Do not paraphrase these names to avoid the word "lease"; doing so strips the legal precision the KO needs.
- **Operational responsibility language**: "Dry lease arrangement" is acceptable when describing the government's operational responsibilities (crewing, maintenance, insurance, basing) under the DPP. This is industry-standard terminology and the HADES RFI uses it comfortably.
- **Investor-facing content**: When speaking to institutional investors, private credit funds, insurance capital, or capital partners, "aircraft lease" or "equipment lease" is the closest market analog and the understood framing. The relevant analog is a commercial airline lease, not a "government lease." Using DPP terminology with this audience creates unnecessary confusion.
- **Actual lease structures in jurisdictions without restrictions**: When LOWI is structuring a traditional lease for a government that does not have lease-related procurement restrictions, use the term accurately.

The wrapper-level suppression and the construct-level permission coexist: a Part 2 section of an RFI response can say "LOWI's Deferred Payment Program can be implemented through a Lease-Purchase, Finance/Capital Lease, or Operating Lease structure" without contradicting the rule, because the DPP is the wrapper and the lease terms name legal forms inside it.

When in doubt about which framing to use, ask which audience the content is for and whether "lease" is being used as a wrapper-level descriptor (suppress) or a legal-construct reference (use directly).

**LOWI is a "financial integrator" and "capital provider."** LOWI both arranges and provides capital. However, LOWI does not operate, maintain, or manage the asset under the DPP (though LOWI can optionally provide operational and maintenance services as a separate engagement — see LOWI's Role below). When describing LOWI's role:
- For government audiences: use "financial integrator" or "capital provider"
- For investor audiences: do NOT use "financial integrator" — this term is meaningless to investors. Instead describe LOWI as "capital provider," "originator," or describe what LOWI does concretely (sources capital, structures programs, manages the capital deployment)
- Avoid language that positions LOWI solely as an "arranger" or solely as an "investor" — LOWI sits in between, both sourcing capital and deploying it

**LOWI does not drive requirements setting or platform selection.** The government identifies what it needs and selects the platform. LOWI finances the acquisition. The analogy is a mortgage broker: you have found the house you want to buy; we provide the financing. A DPP will always cost more than a direct cash purchase, but the benefits (immediate capability, budget flexibility, preserved fiscal space) justify the premium over a lower-cost direct procurement. Make this tradeoff explicit when relevant.

**Do not describe LOWI as a "single counterparty"** or imply it manages the full lifecycle (modifications, maintenance, fleet management) under the DPP. LOWI's DPP role is capital arrangement and provision. Operational and MX services are available separately.

**Avoid "asset-backed leasing"** in new government-facing content. Legacy references in older materials may use it, but new content should use "Deferred Payment Program" or "dry finance structure" for government audiences.

**Be careful with "not debt" framing.** In sales and marketing contexts, do not describe the DPP as "not debt" or contrast "borrowing" with "not borrowing." The nuance matters: sovereign debt constrains future fiscal flexibility and creates obligations that weigh on growth; private credit deployed through a DPP puts existing capital to productive use and generates returns. State what each financing path does on its own terms. In thesis-level or policy writing where the distinction between debt paths is the analytical point, comparative framing is appropriate.

**Do not reference "government co-investment"** in DPP structures. This concept does not exist in LOWI's model. The government is the client making payments; it is not a co-investor.

**Be judicious with contrast framing.** The strong default is declarative: write like a confident expert who states what something is and lets it stand. Contrast framing should be strongly discouraged in sales, marketing, and capability statement contexts; do not write sentences like "This isn't X. It's Y." or set up one thing as a foil for another to position LOWI. If there is a real tension (e.g., DPPs cost more than a direct purchase), address it directly as its own statement; do not bury it in a subordinate clause or use it to set up a contrast. However, contrast framing is appropriate in policy papers, white papers, and thesis-level analytical content where the comparison between financing structures or capital deployment models is the core argument. The "Borrowing from the Past" thesis and the Dual Dividend framework are inherently comparative; contrasting private capital with sovereign debt or taxation is the substance of the argument in those contexts, not a rhetorical device. The test: is the contrast the analytical point being made, or is it a positioning tactic? The former is fine; the latter should be avoided.

**SPV structure language should be kept open.** SPVs are not always structured as trusts. When referencing the legal vehicle, say "special purpose vehicle" or "SPV" without specifying the entity type unless the specific deal requires it.

## The Deferred Payment Program (DPP)

### The Problem LOWI Solves

The DPP is LOWI's product; the problem it solves is structural. Governments routinely face acquisitions they have already decided they want, against budgets that cannot absorb the full cost in a single fiscal year. The constraint is rarely sovereign creditworthiness or political will; it is the mismatch between capital-intensive acquisitions and annual appropriations. Procurement calendars, color-of-money restrictions, ceiling constraints, and competing line items compound the problem. The DPP mobilizes private capital to bridge this mismatch, allowing the government to receive the asset immediately and pay over a program term that aligns with its budgetary reality.

This framing is the analytical point of the offering. Future iterations of this skill should deepen the articulation of the underlying budgetary problems (color-of-money constraints, political procurement friction, allocation mismatches between operating and capital budgets) so that DPP content can diagnose the specific constraint a given acquisition is running into and position the program against it precisely.

**Internal mental model, never external**: LOWI's role maps conceptually to consumer financing platforms like Klarna or Afterpay, extended to sovereign acquisition. This analogy is useful internally for positioning conversations and product thinking; it must never appear in external content, whether investor-facing, government-facing, or marketing. The analogy diminishes the seriousness of the offering in any external context.

### What It Is

LOWI's DPP is a dry finance structure arranged as a pay-to-own agreement between a sovereign government and a private capital provider. The government receives the asset on day one, operates it as its own, and makes fixed periodic payments over the program term. At maturity, title transfers to the government for a nominal amount.

The program term is deliberately shorter than the useful life of the asset. The goal is to transfer full ownership to the military or government quickly and efficiently; not to stretch payments across the platform's entire service life.

The DPP is the wrapper. The underlying legal form can be any of several structures; see the DPP Structural Options section. LOWI is agnostic among them and will go with the structure the client prefers or the one most likely to close the deal, subject to local defense acquisition regulations which often determine which structures are even available.

### How It Works

LOWI finances the acquisition of capital equipment at the direction of its government clients, sourcing capital through institutional partners (private credit funds, pension systems, sovereign wealth funds, and insurance capital). The asset is purchased, modified to the government's mission specification where applicable, and delivered directly to the end user. The program term, payment schedule, and title transfer terms are all tailored to the specific program.

LOWI does not drive requirements setting or platform selection. The government determines what it needs. LOWI's value is financing the acquisition once the requirement and platform have been identified.

### LOWI's Role

Under the DPP, LOWI serves as the financial integrator and capital provider: arranging and deploying the capital structure that connects institutional capital to a sovereign defense acquisition. The underlying arrangement is a dry capital structure; the government operates, maintains, and commands the asset, while LOWI ensures the financing is structured to meet both the capital provider's return requirements and the government's budgetary constraints.

**Optional services beyond the DPP**: LOWI can optionally fulfill operational and maintenance needs for the government, but this is a separate engagement over and above the DPP. The DPP itself is purely a dry finance structure. When describing LOWI's role, always default to the DPP (dry finance) framing unless specifically discussing these optional additional services.

### Ownership Framing: COGO and GOGO

LOWI's default ownership framing is **COGO: Contractor-Owned, Government-Operated**. LOWI (or the SPV) holds legal title during the program term; the government operates the asset as its own from day one. Title transfers to the government at the end of the program term or earlier through buyout or prepayment. COGO is the accurate description of the arrangement for most audiences and most jurisdictions and should be the default term in government-facing prose.

**GOGO: Government-Owned, Government-Operated** is reserved for solicitations where the KO has asked for a GOGO outcome and LOWI needs to present the DPP as GOGO-compatible. The HADES RFI is the precedent: LOWI framed the DPP as GOGO-with-intent by emphasizing the progressive-ownership mechanic (the Army's ownership interest grows with each scheduled payment, culminating in full legal ownership) and presenting the contractor-held title as a facilitation device for the deferred payment rather than a standing ownership claim. This is a lexical contortion that works when the ownership triangle can be argued; do not reach for GOGO as a default.

Use COGO unless the solicitation or client has specifically asked for a GOGO outcome.

### Buyout Provisions

The government can buy out the asset at any time during the program term. The buyout price is the straight-line amortized value based on the deal term (e.g., on a 10-year term, straight-line amortization to $1), plus any associated early termination fees as specified in the contract. This provides a clean exit path and allows governments to accelerate ownership transfer when budgets permit.

### Defaults Are Overridable

The standard program parameters in this skill ($150M minimum program size, 10-year term, 5 to 20-year range, 14% minimum simple-interest rate, fee and termination structures, and the default termination and prepayment modes described in the Base Financial Case) are defaults, not fixed rules. LOWI structures on a program-by-program basis. Any parameter can be overridden by Conrad, Lindsey, or the deal team for a specific program if the economics, the client's constraints, or the competitive situation call for it. When drafting, use the defaults unless told otherwise; when a human overrides, carry the override forward consistently across the document.

### Present Defaults Only Unless Directed

The skill should recognize and retain the full set of options (the five structural options, Termination Modes A/B/C, Prepayment Modes P1/P2/P3, and any future alternatives) so it can recall them for discussion, brainstorming, internal modeling, or a directly queried comparison. In government-facing output, however, the skill should present only the default for each parameter unless Conrad or the deal team has specifically directed otherwise or the solicitation has asked for alternatives. Presenting the full menu of termination premiums or prepayment schedules in a term sheet, RFI response, or proposal surrenders negotiating posture; the alternatives are tools LOWI holds in reserve, not a published menu. The rule applied by context:

- **Government-facing drafts** (term sheets, tender responses, proposals, briefing documents): present the default only. For termination, this is Mode A; for prepayment, P1; for structure, whichever of the five the deal team has selected for this program.
- **Internal models and deal team work product**: carry all options and model them as variants.
- **Direct queries or brainstorming sessions**: recall and discuss any of the options as requested.

When Conrad or Lindsey directs a non-default selection for a specific program, carry that selection as the parameter for the draft and do not reintroduce the default.

## DPP Structural Options

The DPP is the wrapper; the underlying legal form is implemented through one of several structures. Five are canonical in LOWI's current articulation, carried directly from the HADES RFI response and treated as the current model. The list is non-exclusive; LOWI adds or subtracts structures dynamically based on what is known about the counterparty's legal flexibility and local defense acquisition regulations. LOWI is agnostic among them and will go with the structure the client prefers, the structure the client's regime permits, or the structure most likely to get the deal across the finish line.

The DPP wrapper remains constant regardless of the underlying legal form. The five structures share the same economics (deferred payment against initial capital outlay, periodic payments over the program term, path to full government ownership) and differ in how title is held during the term, how the government's ownership interest accretes, and how the end-of-term disposition is mechanically achieved.

This section provides enough depth for menu presentation in formal tenders. The financial modeling of each structure (how interest accrues, how payments amortize, how early termination and prepayment interact with each form) is a cross-dependency with the forthcoming `lowi-financial-models` skill and should be developed there rather than invented here.

**Presenting the menu in official tenders.** Local defense acquisition regulations often constrain which structures are available to a given client; a menu presentation should acknowledge this and, where possible, flag which structures the LOWI team believes are viable given what is known about the counterparty's legal regime. When the five are presented in an RFI, RFP, RFQ, or Sources Sought response, use the structure names directly as terms of art; see the Terminology Rules section on lease carve-outs.

**Do not flag tradeoffs, pitfalls, or sensitivities in menu-level drafting.** The structure descriptions should carry mechanics and fit-to-use statements only. The goal of a menu paragraph is to create pathways for counterparties who are already aware of the tradeoffs between structures to self-select toward the one that fits their regime, not to educate a less sophisticated counterparty into objections before LOWI is at the table. Accounting implications, balance-sheet treatment, sovereign-debt classification concerns, political sensitivities, and similar tradeoffs surface in negotiation, where LOWI can address them in context; they do not belong in the first paragraph a KO reads. This discipline is the general default for first-contact and menu-level drafting across the skill; calling skills with richer context (a known counterparty, a specific acquisition posture, a counterparty-raised concern) can pull in deeper framing through direct query.

### Service Agreement

Under a service agreement construct, LOWI acquires the asset from the OEM and provides asset access to the government for a fixed periodic payment (typically annual or quarterly). The government retains full operational control and operates the asset with government crews and personnel for the duration of the agreement. At the conclusion of the agreed payment term, title transfers to the government program of record.

The distinctive feature of the Service Agreement is that the arrangement is documented as a service contract rather than a financing or lease instrument. This structure is typically appropriate where the client's procurement regime accommodates service contracts more readily than capital leases or direct financing, or where a "service" framing sidesteps capital-budget restrictions that would otherwise constrain the acquisition. End-of-term title transfer is deterministic; the structure is designed to end in government ownership.

### Lease-Purchase

Under a Lease-Purchase structure, LOWI funds the acquisition of the asset and structures recurring payments so the asset is effectively paid down over the agreed term. The government operates the asset from delivery forward. Ownership transfers automatically at the completion of the scheduled payment period; no separate buyout election is required to achieve title transfer.

The distinctive feature of Lease-Purchase is its closeness to a traditional amortizing loan with a deterministic ownership outcome. There is no optionality at end of term to exercise or decline; the government's progressive ownership interest accrues with each scheduled payment and culminates in full title transfer. This structure is typically appropriate where the client's jurisdiction permits lease-to-own mechanics and the client wants a clean, non-optional path to ownership. The HADES RFI defaults to this structure in its Section 6 financial illustration, which makes it the de facto precedent for current government-facing articulations.

### Finance/Capital Lease

Under a Finance/Capital Lease construct, LOWI provides the upfront capital for the acquisition and retains title during the payment term while the government exercises full operational control. The payment structure is designed to produce a defined end-of-term ownership outcome through title transfer at the scheduled end of term; buyout prior to end of term is available on a pre-agreed schedule, typically a 10-year straight-line amortization to $1 with any associated early termination fees as specified in the contract.

The distinctive feature of Finance/Capital Lease is that it maps to the classic finance-structure template familiar to acquisition counsel and finance ministries in most jurisdictions, with LOWI holding title through the payment term as security for the capital provided. This structure is typically appropriate where the client's legal regime recognizes capital-lease instruments as an acquisition tool and the government wants a conventional financing form applied to the program.

### Operating Lease

Under an Operating Lease structure, LOWI funds the asset and provides usage rights to the government for a defined period without obligating the government to a mandatory buyout at end of term. The government retains the option to purchase the asset through a predetermined buyout schedule or return the asset at the end of the period.

The distinctive feature of the Operating Lease is its flexibility and its separation of access from ownership. It is the only one of the five structures that does not presume eventual title transfer; the government can return the asset, buy it out, or extend the arrangement. This structure is typically appropriate where the client's requirement is for capability access over a defined period rather than ownership, where fleet-adjustment flexibility is a priority, or where the political decision to own can or must be deferred. It is also the structure most compatible with short-term capability requirements (sub-decade) where title transfer is not the central objective.

### Secured Financing Agreement

Under a Secured Financing Agreement structure, LOWI provides capital for the acquisition and documents the arrangement as a secured transaction rather than a direct borrowing instrument or a lease. The government carries title to the asset from day one and operates it throughout the payment term; LOWI holds a lien on the asset as security for the capital provided. At the end of the term, the lien is released and the government's ownership becomes unencumbered.

The distinctive feature of the Secured Financing Agreement is that the government holds title from day one, with LOWI's capital protected by a security interest rather than by retained title or lease rights. This sidesteps lease-restriction regimes entirely because no lease exists. This structure is typically appropriate where the client's procurement or legal regime prohibits leases and lease-to-own mechanics but permits secured financing, or where day-one title transfer is a political or legal requirement.

## Base Financial Case

LOWI's general minimum program size is approximately $150 million USD. Standard term is 10 years, with a minimum of 5 years and maximum of 20 years.

### Standard DPP Structure (Base Case)

The base case is a 10-year Deferred Payment Program. Assets are amortized over the 10-year term to $1 using a straight-line methodology. The interest/fee component is calculated on a simple interest basis at a minimum of 14% annually (and up from there depending on contract structure); it does not compound down with the amortization, which keeps government payments flat and predictable across the program term.

The major driver of the interest rate is the duration and structure of the contract. A rolling one-year contract with annual renewal and cancellation rights will carry a higher interest rate than a long-duration (e.g., 10-year) contract with strong cancellation clauses, because the shorter commitment carries more risk for the capital provider.

The program is structured as a rolling one-year contract to align with government appropriation cycles and simplify the budgetary approval process for the client.

### Payment Calculation

For a $120 million asset on the standard 10-year DPP at 15% interest:

- **Amortization**: $120M ÷ 10 years = $12M per year
- **Interest**: $120M × 15% = $18M per year
- **Annual DPP payment**: $12M + $18M = **$30M per year**, plus associated fees (see Fee Structure below)

The interest is calculated on the original capital amount and does not compound down with the amortization. This keeps the annual payment flat and predictable.

LOWI's DPP payments do not include maintenance reserves or insurance. This is a dry structure; the operator handles all maintenance and insurance costs directly.

### Fee Structure

**One-time origination fees (paid at closing):**
These fees are paid by the investors / capital providers, not by the government client. They are pass-through expenses based on actual costs incurred:
- LOWI origination fee: 2% to 4% of capital raised (pass-through)
- Legal fees: $10M to $25M (pass-through)
- Banking fees: 1% to 2% of capital raised (pass-through)

**Ongoing management fees (charged to the government):**
- LOWI management fee: 1% to 4% of the monthly DPP payment, capped at $30M per year annually
  - 1% on larger contracts, 4% on smaller contracts
  - Floor: $600,000 per year
- This is a pass-through expense, not a profit center. It covers the cost of managing the capital structure and contract administration.

### Government-Facing Terminology Equivalents

The economics above are expressed in LOWI's internal and investor-facing vocabulary: "interest" on the original capital, "management fee" on the monthly payment. For formal tender responses (RFIs, RFPs, RFQs, Sources Sought) and other government-facing documents where the Army and other defense customers expect procurement-flavored language, LOWI has used an alternative vocabulary carried through the HADES RFI response and treated here as a named alternative:

- **Interest** (default) becomes **Service Fee** (government-facing alternative)
- **Management fee** (default) becomes **Administrative Fee** (government-facing alternative)

**The rates are equivalencies, not separate numbers.** A 15% interest rate is directly equivalent to a 15% Service Fee at the same underlying economics; the vocabulary changes but the number does not. The default posture for any given transaction is a single rate expressed in whichever vocabulary fits the audience. The two-paragraph articulation in HADES (15% for 5-year, 17% for 10-year, 19% for 15-year as a percent of total program cost, and 2.2% Administrative Fee as a percent of combined Deferred Purchase Payment plus Service Fee) is a specific published illustration for that solicitation and should not be treated as a general rate schedule.

**Vocabulary for the base on which the rate is calculated.** The principal or capital amount on which the Service Fee or interest is computed also carries audience-appropriate vocabulary. In government-facing documents, reference this as **Program Value**, **Total DPP Value**, or **Capital Outlay**. In investor-facing documents, reference it as **Deployed Capital**, **Capital Outlay**, or **Government Program Value**. **Capital Outlay** sits comfortably in either context and can be used as a bridging term when a single document or conversation spans both audiences. Default to the vocabulary that is most fluent for the specific audience unless a human has directed otherwise or a term of art is already in play in the governing document.

**When to use which vocabulary.** Default to the internal vocabulary (interest, management fee) in investor materials, internal strategy documents, white papers, marketing prose, and ordinary commercial discussions. Swap to the government-facing alternative (Service Fee, Administrative Fee) in formal tender responses and contract-facing documents where the KO and contracting staff will read the language in procurement terms. Do not mix the two inside a single document; pick one vocabulary for the document and carry it consistently.

**When rates diverge between audiences.** The standing assumption is that the government-facing rate and the investor-facing rate are the same number expressed in different vocabulary. A deliberate divergence (for example, a 14% investor rate alongside a 15% Service Fee to the government for negotiating margin, or two genuinely different transactions) must be directed explicitly by Conrad or the deal team; do not invent divergence in drafts. When directed, carry the divergence through consistently and note it in internal notes so it does not propagate silently.

### Funding Scope

LOWI finances 100% of program costs by default. The government can make a down payment of up to 20% of the contract value if it wants to reduce the annual DPP payment; LOWI finances a minimum of 80% of the contract. The default is full (100%) financing.

### Capital Structure

Debt is raised at 100% of contract value, with a 3%–10% equity layer beneath the debt. The equity layer functions as a non-payment and late-payment reserve (typically equivalent to six months of DPP payments). The equity cushion is 3% on larger deals and 10% on smaller deals.

### Deposit / Reserve Structure

LOWI maintains a deposit reserve, typically equivalent to six months of DPP payments, held against non-payment and late-payment risk. This is funded from the equity layer. The DPP payments themselves do not include maintenance reserves or insurance; the government handles those directly as part of the dry structure.

### IRR Disclosure Rules

**IRRs are investor-only information.** Never disclose, reference, or discuss IRRs (internal rates of return) in government-facing content. Governments are clients; they do not need to see the return structure.

When discussing IRRs in investor-facing content, always distinguish between:
- **Equity IRR**: Return to the equity layer
- **Debt IRR**: Return to the debt providers
- **Blended IRR**: Weighted return across the capital structure

Do not present a single undifferentiated "IRR" figure. The capital structure has distinct layers with distinct return profiles.

### Minimum Capital Return Constraint

LOWI's capital stack is underwritten to a minimum return of 1.25x on invested capital. This is the floor that governs whether a given program can be structured at all, and it drives the minimum-term logic used in prepayment (see below) and in the shorter-duration variants of the termination modes. Any set of parameters (rate, term, prepayment schedule, termination mechanics) proposed for a deal must clear this floor before it is presented.

This concept is opaque to both government and investor audiences unless a specific counterpart requests it, and even then it is discussed conceptually rather than as a specific multiple. In government-facing output, do not reference a minimum return multiple, a capital-return target, or any derivative of the 1.25x figure; it is an internal underwriting constraint, not a marketing point. Investors understand that LOWI's capital seeks a return; they do not need the specific multiple disclosed as part of a standard pitch. When the concept must be discussed externally (Conrad or Lindsey has directed the disclosure), describe it as "LOWI's capital providers require a minimum return on invested capital that governs our structuring flexibility" without the specific multiple.

For official submissions (term sheets, tender responses, proposals) where the defaults are being committed to paper, the skill should either run a high-level math verification that the default parameters clear the 1.25x floor for the specific deal parameters (capital amount, term, rate, any overrides) or defer to a full financial model if `lowi-financial-models` has been built for the specific transaction. If the verification cannot be completed in-skill, flag it explicitly for Conrad or Lindsey before the submission goes out.

### Termination Modes

Termination mechanics are an evolving area of LOWI's structuring. The direction of travel is toward deals priced without standard penalty structures, reflecting LOWI's preference for commercial flexibility. For now, the skill carries three named termination modes; the human can select whichever fits the program.

**Mode A (default): Conservative premium.** If the government terminates the contract early and retains the asset, a 20% premium is applied on top of the remaining amortized value. If the asset is returned to LOWI (not the preferred outcome), the premium increases to 30% on top of remaining amortized value. If the asset is returned, LOWI facilitates secondary market sale or lease; proceeds above the remaining obligation may revert to the government but this is a negotiated point and not guaranteed.

**Mode B (alternative): HADES-style minimum return floor.** If the government elects to terminate within the first five years of the contract term, LOWI requires a minimum return equal to 150% of the total capital outlay requested of the program if the government retains the asset, and 130% if the asset is returned. Beginning in the sixth year of the period of performance, the government can buy out on the defined buyout schedule; the termination-premium mechanic drops away and the transaction moves to the buyout track. This is the articulation published to the Army in the HADES RFI and carries current-precedent weight in ongoing solicitations.

**Mode C (alternative): Flexible, negotiated per deal.** No stated standard penalty; termination mechanics are negotiated on a program-by-program basis at contracting. This mode aligns with the direction of travel and the commercial flexibility LOWI prides itself on.

The default for skill output is Mode A unless a specific deal context calls for B or C. Modes B and C require further financial modeling before they can be deployed confidently across the full range of program sizes and asset classes; that modeling work is a dependency for `lowi-financial-models` and should be developed there rather than inferred here.

### Prepayment

Prepayment mechanics allow the government to accelerate buyout and reduce the outstanding obligation during the program term. The skill carries three named prepayment modes.

**P1 (default): Up to 10% per year with notice, subject to a dynamic minimum term.** The government may elect to prepay up to 10% of the outstanding contract value per year, reducing the buyout value and timeframe of the program. Standard notice period is 180 days for a prepayment election.

The minimum term during which prepayment is not permitted scales with the overall program term, driven by the 1.25x minimum capital return constraint described above. A 5-year program carries a 2 to 3-year minimum before prepayment may be exercised; a 10-year program carries a 3 to 5-year minimum; longer programs scale proportionally. Within the minimum-term window, termination mechanics (not prepayment) govern if the government wishes to exit early. The specific minimum term for a given deal is set by the deal team when the financial parameters are locked; the skill should use the low end of the range (2 years for a 5-year deal, 3 years for a 10-year deal) as the default placeholder in drafts and flag that the exact minimum term depends on the final rate, capital amount, and return math.

**P2 (alternative): Full prepayment negotiated.** No standard cap; prepayment schedule, notice, and any fee implications are negotiated per deal at contracting.

**P3 (alternative): Not contemplated in default structure.** The program does not contemplate prepayment; if the client raises the issue, it becomes a negotiated point at contracting.

The default for skill output is P1 unless a specific deal context calls for P2 or P3.

### Flexibility

LOWI can structure the DPP and amortization period from 5 to 20 years depending on the client's utilization requirements and preferences. The interest rate is similarly flexible (minimum 14%, adjusted based on contract duration and structure). These are negotiated on a program-by-program basis.

### Cost Scope

LOWI's fees are not inclusive of operating costs. This is a dry finance structure; the government bears all operating, maintenance, insurance, and mission costs. LOWI's fees cover the capital arrangement and provision only.

### Asset Scope

LOWI's value proposition is solving structural budgetary problems through the efficient deployment of private capital; the DPP mechanism is applicable to essentially any capital asset a sovereign acquires. The scope runs from consumables and personal equipment (boots, body armor) through tactical systems (small unmanned aerial systems, communications equipment) to strategic platforms (aircraft, satellites, naval vessels). In external materials the category is labeled "Executive Aircraft"; "Majesty" is LOWI's internal shorthand that preserves the 4-M alliteration.
- **Military Aircraft**: Dedicated military-certified aircraft, including multi-role transport and training aircraft, sourced to meet specific defense requirements.

When writing capability content for aviation-focused audiences, the 4 Ms are the preferred organizing structure. When writing broadly about LOWI's offering, lead with the financial value proposition and the asset-agnostic framing, then reference aviation as the core expertise rather than the exclusive scope.

**Illustrative (non-exclusive) asset categories beyond aircraft.** The DPP applies to any capital asset a sovereign acquires; the following list is illustrative and should not be read as a bounded scope:

## Core Thesis: "Borrowing from the Past, Not from the Present or the Future"

This is LOWI's central intellectual framework. The argument:

Governments fund capital programs through taxation and sovereign debt, both of which impose real economic costs. Tax revenue directed to defense acquisition is capital extracted from the productive economy; sovereign debt creates obligations that weigh on future growth and constrain fiscal flexibility for generations.

There is a third path: mobilizing existing private capital. This approach "borrows from the past" by deploying capital already accumulated by institutional investors, sovereign wealth funds, pension systems, and insurance capital. No new obligations are created for current or future taxpayers.

Sovereign debt constrains future budgets and reduces fiscal flexibility. Private credit deployed through a DPP puts existing, already-generated wealth to productive use and generates returns for the capital providers while delivering capability to the government. In sales and marketing contexts, let each statement stand on its own and let the reader draw the conclusion. In policy and thesis-level writing, where the comparison between these paths is the core argument, comparative framing is expected and appropriate.

This framing deliberately moves past the technical "deadweight loss" argument (Feldstein, Chetty) toward a broader, more intuitive case. Do not use "deadweight loss" terminology. In sales and marketing contexts, avoid "X vs. Y" contrast structures and make declarative statements about each path independently. In analytical and policy contexts, direct comparison is part of the argument.

## The Dual Dividend Framework

This is the second major intellectual framework, building on the "Borrowing from the Past" thesis. It describes two dividends that flow from channeling private capital into defense programs:

### Defense Dividend

When citizens invest their own capital in defense programs through pension funds, insurance products, and investment vehicles, they become active participants who have chosen to mobilize their own wealth toward national security. This shift carries real political weight. It removes political friction around defense spending and ties citizens to defense initiatives through positive, patriotic sentiment. The citizen is a stakeholder in national security.

### Economic Dividend

Private capital is return-oriented. It deploys faster and more decisively; governments can commit fully to acquisition programs with confidence. More capital enters the defense industrial base and the broader economy. Key points:

- Private credit structures backing sovereign defense programs offer returns above what government bonds pay at comparable durations; this is essential to the incentive structure
- The private sector can push more money into defense and do it efficiently because the incentive is return
- Both the public and the government can fully realize their acquisition needs
- This is already-generated wealth redeployed productively; it drives the economy forward

## COTS Platform Rationale

LOWI's core competency is commercially available platforms modified for government and military missions (Commercial Off-The-Shelf, or COTS). While the DPP structure applies broadly across any capital asset a sovereign acquires (see Asset Scope), the COTS thesis is strongest in manned aviation and anchors LOWI's deepest relationships and operating experience. The case for COTS platforms:

- **Proven airframes**: Platforms from Gulfstream, Bombardier, Embraer, Textron, Dassault, Airbus, and Boeing with tens of thousands of flight hours and established safety records
- **Commercial supply chains and civilian maintenance centers**: No dependence on bespoke military logistics
- **Large internal cross-sections**: Business jets offer substantial cabin volume for mission systems integration
- **Operational discretion**: These aircraft can operate in civilian airspace and at commercial airports without attracting attention; they "hide in plain sight"
- **Lower and more predictable lifecycle costs**: The focus is on fleet management and lifecycle cost reduction
- **Rapid availability**: Commercial production lines deliver aircraft on established timelines

### Key Proof Points

Refer to `references/proof-points.md` for detailed case studies. Summary:

- **EA-37B Compass Call**: Replaced the EC-130H (C-130 Hercules) with a Gulfstream G550 for electronic attack. 50% cost reduction, 50% weight reduction, crew from 13 to 9, $300M in avoided sustainment costs. The most developed proof point for the COTS thesis.
- **E-11A BACN**: Bombardier Global Express used for Battlefield Airborne Communications Node. 15,500+ combat missions, 98% readiness rate. Demonstrates sustained operational success on a commercial platform.
- **Saab GlobalEye**: Modified Bombardier Global 6000 for airborne early warning & control (AEW&C). Multi-role ISR platform adopted by the UAE.
- **UK FSTA (AirTanker)**: Private capital delivered sovereign military capability at scale; Airbus A330 MRTT fleet under PFI arrangement. This program has been completed and the assets transferred to the military. It is a past proof point demonstrating that private financing of military aviation works, not a current ongoing program.

## Writing Voice for Substantive Content

Conrad's voice in LOWI materials is declarative, factual, and analytical with an edge. Content that has been refined through iteration follows these patterns:

**Do:**
- Make declarative, factual statements; let the reader draw conclusions
- Write like a confident expert. If something is true, say it is true. Let each statement carry its own weight.
- Use longer, structured sentences typical of graduate-level analytical writing
- Present arguments as logical sequences
- Reference specific programs, figures, and citations where available
- Use active, concise language with personal pronouns ("We finance...", "I have structured...", "Our model...")
- Bridge between sections with analytical connective tissue
- If there is a real tension (e.g., cost premium, risk tradeoff), address it directly as its own statement

**Do not:**
- Use tricolons (three-part parallel structures like "faster, cheaper, better") — Conrad flags these as AI fingerprints
- Use effusive framing ("perhaps the most telling case of all," "remarkably," "impressively")
- Use rhetorical questions — they read as soft and evasive in professional defense writing. Make direct statements instead.
- Use contrast framing in sales, marketing, or capability contexts ("Unlike X, Y does Z" / "One does X; the other does Y" / "Not X but Y") — in these contexts, make each statement declarative and self-sufficient. Note: in policy or thesis-level analytical writing where comparing financing paths or capital models is the core argument, comparative framing is appropriate and expected.
- Introduce negatives in sales verbiage ("This isn't X. It's Y.") — go straight to the positive assertion
- Over-use colons and semicolons in sequence; one per sentence is fine, but chains of them (triple-colons) read as AI-generated
- Over-detail examples in summary contexts; name programs and move on, save detail for dedicated sections
- Use editorial commentary where declarative statement would suffice
- Write in a way that reads as selling; the tone is analytical authority, not persuasion

When drafting for Conrad's review, expect multiple rounds. His feedback pattern typically moves from "too AI" → "too effusive" → "tighten up" → approved. Front-load the tightening.
